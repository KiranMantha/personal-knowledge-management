*By Aaron Sung, UX Designer at KaiOS*

I have the habit of checking the weather forecast on my phone every morning. I’m not alone in this: millions of people all around the world do the same.

In the past, we got our forecasts from the TV and the radio; nowadays, we get it from apps, which give us even more information. Through weather apps, we can find out what the weather is like now and what it’s going to be like two hours from now, tomorrow, and next week.

Checking the weather on our phones only takes a minute — or at least that’s what it *should* take — which is why weather app designers need to focus on presenting weather forecasts as clearly as possible. Users should be able to glance at their app and instantly get the information they need.

Designing consistent, friendly, and concise weather icons is a part of that. In this article, I’ll share how these results can be achieved based on my own experience designing weather icons for a KaiOS weather app.

## 1. Consistent

It helps to create a complete optical grid system before designing an icon set. The optical grid helps maintain the unity of the icons and make sizes and spacing consistent.

To design an optical grid system, start with a square — that’s how you define the default icon size and create padding. Then, draw two rectangles to represent the maximum height and width. The optical grid system is now complete!

The next step is to define the basic elements of the icon set: the sun, the moon, clouds, rain, etc.

From these basic elements, we can derive combinations to present diverse weather conditions. This results in efficient production as well as a consistent design style.

## 2. Friendly

Since a significant portion of our customers are first-time mobile phone and internet users, it’s crucial that the icons we create are both friendly and recognizable. We approach this by using gentle colors and round shapes.

In terms of colors, we use those that are close to people’s established impressions. For the weather icons, I used yellow to represent the sun and lightning, blue for clouds and water, and light green for wind. I also used a color overlap effect to make the icons more stylish and visually appealing.

In terms of shapes, I designed the icons based on circles. Round shapes are pleasing to the eye and thus user-friendly.

## 3. Concise

Less isn’t boring — it’s powerful.

I used basic geometric shapes, simplified them, and used as few components as possible. Rather than using complex color effects, I filled the icons with solid colors to make them more recognizable regardless of their size.
