Modern smartphone screens — like those of the iPhone X — can reach up to 6 inches in size, with the average smartphone screen being around 5 inches. This is great for users, who have a better viewing experience, and it’s great for designers because we have more space to work with.

As a designer who has worked on smartphone interfaces for years, the challenge I faced when joining the Kai UX team was a big one: suddenly I had less than 3 inches to work with. Although I’ve had experience designing for smartwatches, designing phone apps for small screens was a whole different story.

To my surprise, working within these constraints actually turned out to be a blessing in disguise. It pushed my creativity and skills as a designer, and I’d like to share with you four learnings from my experience designing for small screens.

## 1. Striking the right balance

Designers need to find balance in showing enough content to avoid an architecture that goes deep in navigation and not clutter the screen. In some cases, this was easy to solve. For example, when designing a picture gallery, we decided to show just three columns of photos instead of two. This way the user sees enough content and at the same time we don’t sacrifice usability.

![]()

## 2.Legibility

When working with smaller screens, legibility of text forms an especially tricky problem. The simplest solution is to increase text size, but on smaller screens, this leads to a new issue: [truncation](https://oleb.net/blog/2015/02/truncated-text/) — the text being cut off — so the entire meaning or function becomes incomprehensible.

Using *All Apps* as an example, you can see the screen looks significantly cleaner with the name of the app in focus on top, which also eliminates risks of truncation.

![]()

## 3. Figuring out the essential

Designing for the small feature phone screen forces us to be concise in our copywriting. *Toasts* are an excellent example of this.

Toasts are the notification messages that pop up at the top or center of your screen to notify you of an event and then they disappear. On the KaiOS smart feature phones they disappear after three seconds so the longer the message, the more likely it won’t be read. It’s crucial to make sure you give out enough information without redundancy, yet at the same time trying to be as clear as possible explaining to users what had just happened. ## 4. Thoughtful touches

To keep KaiOS-powered devices affordable, many features and components that are considered standard on a smartphone cannot be included in our smart feature phone.

An example of this is the accelerometer, a component that is included in any modern smartphone. It helps determine the position and rotation of the device, which is essential to many core features of a mobile operating system.

On KaiOS-powered devices, there is no accelerometer. One of the use cases were this becomes a problem is when the user wants to play a video in full screen. If the video is shot in portrait (vertically), there is no problem, as it matches the resolution of the screen. But if the video is in landscape (horizontal), it leaves big empty spaces at the top and bottom of the video on an already tiny screen.

How did we solve this? We defined a new rule: if the width of the video is greater than its height, the video automatically rotates by 90 degrees, so it’s played full screen without any empty space. The below design shows you how this works.

**A blessing in feature phone disguise**

Designing for smaller screens forced me to revisit the timeless design principle of focusing on the essentials, to keep things simple yet easy to use. The constraints caused by the lack of screen real-estate drove me to continuously think about what really mattered.

While these limitations can be challenging at times, the learnings can be applied broadly. These experiences have made me a better and more focused designer, and I hope you can also apply some of these learnings in your own design projects!
